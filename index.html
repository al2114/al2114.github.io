Luko.hi

Interactive voice-controlled robotic lamp assistant

This is my most recent and on-going personal projects aiming to create a voice-controlled personal assitant robotic lamp running on a RaspberryPi. The project was very much inspired by a TED Talk given by Guy Hoffman, then a researcher from the MIT Media Labs. The primary motivation behind this project is to explore different aspects of human-robot interaction, and how robots may be seemlessly integrated into the human environment. Using a lamp as an interactive medium is very effective at achieving this, as not only is it both able to fit naturally in the household environment, but also has a wide range of motion to be able to perform relatively expressive movements. Additionally, I am also interested in seeing how non-verbal communication can be used to enhance interactivity when it comes to communicating with artificial agents. The project is still work-in-progress. I have currently motorised parts of the lamp and developed a primitive reverse kinematic model which allows for the RaspberryPi to interface with the motor controller to issue basic smooth movement, as well as a very basic voice-controlled agent. Future work include refining the voice agent, implementing some useful functionality, and computer vision for facial and gesture tracking.

This was a project developed in close collaboration with Microsoft as part of my third year group project course. The project was built on top of the Smart Bike Project, an initiative proposed by Microsoft to help Mark Pollock, a blind and paralegic athelete, extend the IoT capabilities of his hand-powered bike and provide assistive technologies to support his physical training. After speaking with Mark Pollock and listening to his experiences as an individual with disabilities, we quickly realised that there exists a problem in the way blind users interact and utilise smartphones and mobile applications; this problem is inherent in the way smartphones and user interfaces are designed. As such, our team proposed a new design model for inclusive interface design which involves hybrid gesture-voice input, intuitive navigation structure, and contextual support to improve user experience. We built a fitness app based on this new porposed model as an alternative solution to Mark's original setup, which required Mark to utilise a traditional GUI fitness app to interface with his bike's sensors. During this project, I had been primarily responsible for project coordination, system architecture and software design. Our team's design was very well received by both Mark, and at Microsoft, with interset gained by Microsoft's Research team to further build on top of this idea.

Eurobot 2017

Task-based robotic competition

I participated in this year's Eurobot competition as a member of the Imperial College Robotic Society team. Eurobot is an annual competition where teams from universities across Europe build robots to compete autonomously against each other by completing tasks and scoring points in a time-limited arena environment. This year, the competition's theme was Moon Village, and the primary task involved gathering and collecting various "lunar objects" within the provided time limit. We decided to challenge ourselves by creating an omnidirectional robot using three omni wheels. The robot utilises a combination of RaspberryPis, Arduinos, and mbeds to drive its various functions including motor control, arm control, localisation, path planning and collision detection. Our robot uses odometry data, computer vision triangulation (using chilitag beacons) and kalman filtering to provide localisation information, which it makes use of to navigate throughout the arena. Utilising a three-wheel omnidirectional drive proved to be challenging, and even after a lot of effort put into tuning motor controllers, still resulted in suboptimal motion when compared to many of the other robots. Nonetheless, using three-wheeled omnidirectional drive had never been seen before on the competition, and our implementation garnered quite a decent amount of interest during the event.

sup

The Plantarium Nova is a temperature and humidity controlled capsule designed for home plant growing and monitoring developed by Team Icarus. The device runs on a ESP8266 WiFi module and interfaces with a series of sensors including LED lights, a temperature sensor, a humidity sensor, a humidifier, and a temperature regulator. This allows for the device to create a closed loop control system to regulate the capsule and provide the optimal climate profile for a given plant. Additionally, the device connects to an MQTT broker which allows for it to communicate with our backend system, enabling IoT functionality. A front-end control panel interface was developed using the D3.js framework to graphically visualise the sensor readings in real time, with the side panel enabling users to create profiles for different temperature/humidity settings. I was primarily responsible for the development of the web control interface and backend system.

Ephemeral

Text messaging application where messages are temporary and keystrokes are sent in real-time between users.

Ephemeral is an app that a few friends and I had worked on during IC Hack 2017. It is a textmessaging platform trying to emulate the way individuals communicate in spoken conversation, where information is streamlined and words are temporary. As such, your message begins appearing on your correspondent's screen as soon as you begin your first keystroke, and subsequently the message will begin to disappear as soon as the full message has been delivered and read. The aim is to create a messaging platform that will allow for more genuine conversations to take place between individual, with the added benefit of privacy offered by typing your conversation (i.e. in public spaces). We also took this as a learning opportunity, and set ourselves the constraint of developing the entire platform using just Swift, utilising Perfect, a server-side Swift framework to develop our app's backend.

As part of my first year project, I developed a realtime object detection implementation on an Altera Cyclone IV FPGA. The implementation was built using Catapult-C HLS, enabling hardware acceleration of a computationally expensive task. Computation involve performing convolution and colour space mapping to threshold out interested colour sections. As skin detection was a feature focused on in this implementation, we utilise chroma-difference to isolate regions of colours by mapping and thresholding in the YCrCb colour space. Users may utilise the cursor to focus on different parts of the image for the system to automatically calibrate thresholding parameters to perform detection of a specific colour profile (e.g. a person's face to detect skin). By paralelising and pipelining the various stages, the system can achieve near-realtime and high throughput result.
